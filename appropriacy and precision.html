IELTS Writing: Appropriacy and Precision

This guide explains the key components of a high-scoring vocabulary for the IELTS Writing test, focusing on appropriacy and precision. It provides scenarios with clear examples to help you understand how to use words correctly and effectively.

1. Choosing the Right Word (Appropriacy)

This is about using words that are suitable for a formal, academic context. Many common words have formal synonyms that are better suited for the test.

Scenario: Formal vs. Informal Language

Good Example: The government should implement stricter policies on environmental protection.

"Implement" is a formal, academic word that is highly appropriate here.

Bad Example: The government should get stricter policies on environmental protection.

"Get" is too informal and lacks the specific meaning of putting a plan into action.

Scenario: Avoiding Overly Casual Phrasal Verbs

Good Example: The study indicates that the trend is likely to continue in the future.

"Continue" is a precise and formal verb.

Bad Example: The study indicates that the trend is likely to go on in the future.

"Go on" is a casual phrasal verb. While its meaning is clear, it is not appropriate for an academic essay.

2. Using Words Precisely (Precision)

This is about using words with their correct meaning and in the correct context. A word might be formal, but if it doesn't fit the context, it won't score well.

Scenario: Connotation (Positive vs. Negative)

Good Example: The company's new strategy was a prudent decision, leading to long-term success.

"Prudent" has a positive connotation, meaning wise and careful, which fits the context of a successful decision.

Bad Example: The company's new strategy was a calculating decision, leading to long-term success.

"Calculating" has a negative connotation, suggesting cold and selfish motives, which contradicts the positive outcome.

Scenario: Specificity and General Words

Good Example: The disparity in income between the rich and the poor is a growing concern.

"Disparity" is a precise word that specifically means a great difference, which is perfect for this context.

Bad Example: The difference in income between the rich and the poor is a growing concern.

"Difference" is too general. While not incorrect, it lacks the precision and impact of "disparity."

Scenario: Correct Collocation

Collocation refers to words that naturally go together. Using the wrong combination sounds unnatural and is considered an error.

Good Example: The government must bear the responsibility for the economic crisis.

"Bear the responsibility" is a strong and natural collocation in English.

Bad Example: The government must take the responsibility for the economic crisis.

While grammatically correct, "take" is a less common verb for this noun and sounds unnatural. The correct collocation is "take responsibility for."

3. Choosing Academic Vocabulary

A key part of appropriacy is using vocabulary that is suitable for an academic essay. This means avoiding colloquialisms and using formal language.

Scenario: Avoiding Colloquialisms

Good Example: The research paper investigated the effects of social media on mental health.

"Investigated" is a formal, academic verb.

Bad Example: The research paper checked out the effects of social media on mental health.

"Checked out" is a colloquial phrase, completely inappropriate for a formal essay.
